## Key Topics:
- Optimal amplification ratios for Bitcoin treasury companies using digital credit (preferred equity structures)
- Why preferred equity without debt is safer than debt-based leverage for Bitcoin exposure
- Bitcoin's current bear market context and the case for maximum amplification at current prices
- What drives liquidity and trading volume in Bitcoin treasury equities
- Strive's Zeta digital credit instrument: daily dividends, par pegging, and market reception
- The path to a mature digital credit ETF ecosystem requiring 30–40 issuers
- DeFi and tokenization as a major demand driver for digital credit, especially for overseas stablecoin users
- Digital credit as the corporate hurdle rate vs. Bitcoin as the hurdle rate for high-conviction holders
- Fed policy outlook, inflation, AI-driven deflation, and the macro case for Bitcoin breaking out in 12–18 months
- The bifurcation between companies with genuine Bitcoin conviction vs. those treating it as a get-rich-quick scheme

(0:00:38) Let's dive right in. We were speaking with Fang a few minutes ago and I asked him what he thought the maybe higher echelon of amplification ratio might be. You know, right now, correct me if I'm wrong, Strive's in the low 40s, 42, 45, 42.5 — I'm sure it changes every day. What are you seeing in the market and what do you see as something that you're comfortable with, that's responsible? Does the market decide? And I'll give you the TLDR — I don't know if you saw the interview, he said 50 to 60%, then he said 40 to 60% plus. So what are your thoughts on financial engineering, a term that we've used a lot today?

**Matt Cole:**
(0:01:28) Yeah, I actually did catch the interview. I think he might have also said that if the market was comfortable, that if digital credit was pegged at 100, even higher than 60% could make sense. And so I think a couple things are true. One, that when you don't have debt, when your balance sheet is debt-free, and you're amplifying with preferred equity only, that from a risk management perspective — especially with Bitcoin, as we're talking right now, Bitcoin's down on the day, it's 72,700, the 200-week moving average is not much further below this. I think we've been in a bear market — whatever your comfortable range of amplification is, you should want it to be at the highest end of that range when Bitcoin is really cheap. And I think right now Bitcoin is really cheap. And so when you actually run all the different Monte Carlo simulations — thousands of different simulations of how does it break from a credit perspective — the amplification could push much higher than 60% if you have a cash reserve in place, if you assume that the current Bitcoin bear market will not be deeper or longer in duration than prior Bitcoin bear markets. And so right now the current Bitcoin bear market has been a very mild one. The sentiment might be low, but the lowest Bitcoin's been from its previous all-time high right now has been about 50%. Historically it's been 80% plus drawdowns, and I think that's consistent Tim with what you and I on the Hurdle Rate have been talking about — this concept that we're probably more in a super cycle right now than we are in a typical Bitcoin bear winter. I'm skeptical that we see 80% drawdowns in Bitcoin maybe ever again, but definitely not in this bear market. And so if you have that belief and you think that likely the Bitcoin bottom is in — but even if the Bitcoin bottom isn't in, that we don't go much lower than the previous low — then you would want to be amplified to the max right now. So I think even a 70–80% plus amplification ratio with preferred equity would not be something that I think a company would struggle to get through from a credit perspective. The flip side of that is that may not be true from a market perspective. The market may not let a company go that high before it starts pricing in concerns on either the common equity or the preferred equity. This is where you really start getting into more of the art than the science. We are underwriting a perpetual bull thesis in Bitcoin with digital credit, and that flywheel is most successful if the preferred equity investor has confidence in the security — that it's trading well from a credit perspective — and that the common equity has confidence in the company that it'll be able to manage those liabilities over the long run. So you have to manage both of those stakeholders very seriously. What have we seen in the market to date? I think Andrew Webley has covered this really well, but the market has rewarded, generally speaking, higher amplification — not just with a higher EV multiple, but also a higher market cap multiple. They view higher amplification as more bullish if they think that the company is going to be able to survive and thrive with that. We see that across the industry right now. So that's one data point that suggests the market is comfortable with higher amplification ratios. The second is that you look at Zeta as an example — it's paying at par right now. We're moving towards daily dividends, and we obviously have a higher amplification ratio than Strategy, but Zeta's liquidity on a relative basis — for one share of Zeta, how liquid is it? — is actually more liquid than STRK. Since we've announced daily dividends, it's been pegging at par more frequently, and we have higher amplification. So maybe amplification ratio is not the number one concern for the investor in digital credit. Maybe they're more concerned with dividend frequency, they're more concerned with the yield, and still underwriting a general belief that Bitcoin will go up into the future. Right now with Strive's amplification ratio at 45% plus, we're very active in the markets right now — I guess you'll see next week what our new amplification ratio is — but the market's pricing in a lot of confidence in that. And I'm glad to see that because Tim, you've obviously been part of the True North crowd that I think is doing institutional-grade analysis on digital credit, and they've gone deep into how hard it is to break these structures. And I think the market is saying they agree with that analysis. So all that to say — that was a super long answer — 60% amplification doesn't even give me heartburn at all, but we will also care what the market's perception is of that as we manage our risk.

**Matt Cole:**
(0:07:49) I think it's mostly driven by capital structure and belief in creating a flywheel to amplify and outperform Bitcoin over time. What I mean by that is you look at Strive and our common equity, which I think is probably the most liquid per Bitcoin owned in the ecosystem right now. We like to joke about podcasters per share — I think that's going to age into like an epic meme that if you were there you'll know, but for those that aren't there will just think we're crazy that podcasters in and of themselves drive value — but it ultimately is the structure itself. What do I think investors want? I think investors want an understanding that there's not some debt term that causes potential liquidation risk. I think people get — I would say almost overly concerned with that relative to the actual risk. Which kind of gets into, you know, when Strive had — we talked about this on the Hurdle Rate, Tim — when Strive had literally less than a million dollars of debt remaining and I was so focused on retiring that last $800,000 of debt, because I knew it would just live rent-free more than it should in people's minds. So I think that causes almost irrational fear. But they want amplification — effectively levered Bitcoin exposure without the ability to get liquidated to the downside. I think that's really important for people and I think that's most effectively done through digital credit. As other players enter the digital credit ecosystem in the Bitcoin treasury landscape, I think the market will reward that to the extent that they can get liquidity in the digital credit instrument. So that's going to be providing something that the market wants — that could be a digital credit that pays in the pound, one that pays in the euro, in the yen. Obviously there are different regulations. But just at a high level, I think about digital credit as — it's obviously not a money market account, that's kind of the first thing you see on our website — but I do think that digital credit if designed well can be better than a money market account, and the demand for that can be exponentially high. I think it's going to be north of $3 trillion pretty easily in the next 10 years. When you think about something that is that large of an opportunity, it makes sense for there to be more than one issuer. Right now there's two — I think it makes sense for there to be much more than two — and for each issuer to really think about what is the value proposition that I'm bringing that's different from the ones already out there. There are so many different ways that could go — it could be what you pay in. For Strive, we took the approach of being the perpetual Bitcoin bull: we're going to run fast with high amplification, and we're going to pay a higher yield on the digital credit. We think there was going to be demand on both sides, and that thesis is playing out. But I think there's a lot of other very coherent theses that will have a lot of demand and a digital credit instrument that will power a Bitcoin treasury company. It's going to be very interesting to see what different players come up with. There's a lot of demand for more players, and we want to be helpful in getting more players out there because it obviously helps us too — to have a thriving digital credit ecosystem, to have a thriving Bitcoin treasury ecosystem.

**Matt Cole:**
(0:12:24) On the ETF side, you probably need 30 to 40 issuers really to say you have a mature industry. There are workarounds in ETF land through things like swaps where you can create ETF products with fewer issuers. We announced a digital credit ETF that's effectively a levered version of something that will hold both SATA and STRK, and that actually came from talking to financial advisors and asking them what type of ETF they would like to see. I'll never forget that it made Michael Saylor laugh when I told him that people want levered exposure to digital credit in the financial advisor space — but that is something that we're putting out there and will allow us to talk digital credit, even just SATA and STRK directly, to about 7,600 advisors that we're connected to through our ETF business. But if it's 30 to 40 issuers needed, that means there needs to be a lot more issuers of digital credit. But if you think about any financial services industry — banking, insurance companies — there are so many different banks, so many different insurance companies. If you asked an average person what's the difference between Bank of America and JP Morgan besides the brand, they couldn't give you an answer. But they're both massive and there's massive demand for them. I think the same is true in the Bitcoin treasury space, which is why it's such a fun space. It's why you have a conference like this with the Smarter Web Company and all sorts of different executives from different Bitcoin treasury companies coming in that want to see Smarter Web win and Smarter Web wants to see them win — it's about growing the pie at this stage and being collaborative, friendly competition sort of thing. I think that will continue for the next decade plus. As far as what we're seeing talking across institutions and the DeFi ecosystem — right now it's been pretty well covered that STRK is about 80% owned by retail, started out smaller. Strive has something similar. I'll say that the data to verify these things is really opaque. As an example, you'll see like a very large holding from Fidelity for Zeta, and Fidelity we know is part retail and part institution — breaking that out is hard. So there are some data quality issues, but we do know that it started out more institutional on both of them and has moved more retail. That kind of makes sense to me — Bitcoin was a retail phenomenon before institutions even started looking at it, and I think digital credit will be a similar thing. Which leads to the DeFi part of the conversation. I think DeFi is going to be huge for digital credit. It's really not that crazy when you unpack it a little bit — there is demand for hundreds of billions of dollars of stablecoins, and the primary source of that demand is not in a place like the United States. My credit card works, and so for me to go from using my credit card to using a stablecoin, it doesn't really solve a pain point in a lot of people in more developed countries' lives. But in overseas, third-world, authoritarian-nation places, there is a desire to be outside that system and to be in something that's more stable — which was the rise of stablecoins like Tether as the primary example. I think that digital credit is going to compete directly with that. To the extent that those investors — those multi-millionaires, billionaires overseas that are stablecoin buyers — see the ability to get an attractive yield in an instrument that doesn't have a lot of volatility, and they're already on chain so they'll obviously understand things like Bitcoin, I think the demand for that is going to absolutely skyrocket. And unlike an institution, those buyers of stablecoins have an investment committee of one individual. So I think they can move very quickly, and as tokenization happens and makes these products available to them, I think you're going to see very quick demand to continue on chain.

**Tim Kotzman:**
(0:17:04) Matt, many people probably know that we consider Bitcoin to be our hurdle rate, but the conversation has kind of come around to Strategy saying that digital credit is the hurdle rate for corporations. Do you agree with that? And have the conversations you've had with executives changed over time from more of a "you should adopt a Bitcoin treasury strategy" to "you should look at digital credit"? And I would assume the digital credit conversation is easier — but yeah, what has been your experience from your seat with Bitcoin versus digital credit in the public or private sector?

**Matt Cole:**
(0:17:53) It comes down to a couple different things. One of them is your conviction in Bitcoin. What you're seeing right now is kind of a bifurcation of the initial influx of Bitcoin treasury companies — some of them actually did not have conviction in Bitcoin. I think they viewed it as more of a get-rich-quick scheme for their corporations, and you're seeing them capitulate on the strategy. We saw even yesterday a company completely abandon their Bitcoin strategy less than a year after they've done it, probably for no reason other than Bitcoin's in a bear market and they weren't prepared to ride that out. And then you're seeing other companies like Smarter Web Company, like Strive, like Strategy — a handful of others — that are continuing to stack unapologetically, unfazed. For those types of companies, Bitcoin is a better hurdle rate than digital credit, because they actually have the conviction and the ability to stack regardless of the volatility and can take a long-term time horizon. For the average company, they're just trying to meet their bills, they're trying to park their cash to invest in the future, and they're able to go from cash or treasuries to digital credit — going from something that is losing money relative to the rate of debasement of fiat currencies around 6 to 7% a year to something in digital credit that actually is outpacing that. And I think this is the reality right now: fiat currencies are still the reserve currency, the dollar is the reserve currency of the world right now. I think we're in a fourth turning — moving from a system where fiat currencies are the main source of money to a Bitcoin-denominated future — but I think that's going to take probably decades even still, as Bitcoin moves into the millions of dollars per coin. And during that transition, I think digital credit is a great form of effectively money or savings for these corporations where they don't have to underwrite the volatility of Bitcoin, but they can still benefit from this monetary transition instead of being harmed by it.
