**Lance Vitanza:**
(0:03:27) Well, sure. So, from my standpoint, Bitcoin yield serves as the cornerstone of our valuation framework. Bitcoin yield is of course the pace at which Bitcoin per fully diluted share changes over a given period of time, or by virtue of one or more specific transactions. All else equal, investors should want to own the companies that will grow Bitcoin holdings on a per share basis the fastest — that is to say, they will report the highest Bitcoin yield. The premium to underlying Bitcoin holdings at which a company trades — some call that mNAV — that should largely be a function of expected Bitcoin yield over time. So a company that is expected to generate a 30% Bitcoin yield over the next four years should trade at a considerably higher premium to its underlying Bitcoin value than a company that's only expected to generate a 10% Bitcoin yield over the next four years. Of course, all else equal.

(0:04:25) Now, forecasting future Bitcoin yield isn't for the faint of heart, but we think investors can get a beat on that by considering historical Bitcoin yield along with current leverage ratios, capital market conditions, and so forth. And look, the bottom line is: so long as Bitcoin continues to outperform most other asset classes, I think there's going to be a range of positive outcomes for the various public Bitcoin treasury companies, or PBTCs as I like to call them. But all well-run PBTCs should materially outperform spot Bitcoin or spot ETFs, because a well-run PBTC will employ leverage and potentially accretive stock issuance at a premium to NAV in order to grow that Bitcoin per share.

(0:05:14) And I like to put it this way, and I can't emphasize this point enough. If you buy one Bitcoin today and you hold it for 20 years, in 20 years you've got exactly one Bitcoin. If you buy one Bitcoin via an ETF, in 20 years you'll have something like 0.95 Bitcoin because the fees that accumulate are generally paid for by selling Bitcoin. Those fees, by the way, they may not amount to much in any given year, but the impact of course is compounded over time. And my view at least is that anyone interested in Bitcoin is almost by definition concerned with longer rather than shorter time frames. If you want to store value for a week or a month or even a year, go ahead and use the dollar or the pound or whatever. It's those of us that are looking to retain our wealth over generational time frames that are most drawn to Bitcoin. In any case, the fact of the matter is this: if you buy Bitcoin through an ETF, you are contractually guaranteed to own less Bitcoin per share over time.

While we expect Bitcoin will over time become an effective store of value, in the near term adopting Bitcoin as a treasury asset creates an immediate uptick in volatility, which means more volume and more interest in the stock.
