## Key Topics:
- Introduction of Bitcoin treasury companies: Smarter Web Company (UK), Strive (US), and Kraken Institutional
- Core KPIs for valuing Bitcoin treasury companies: mNAV, BTC yield, and company size
- Bitcoin as the hurdle rate — why returns must outperform Bitcoin to justify the strategy
- How Kraken Institutional supports treasury companies via custody, trading, and bespoke lending solutions
- Evolution of capital instruments from convertible notes to perpetual preferred equity
- The "Bitcoin accretive" flywheel: issuing shares, raising capital, buying Bitcoin to grow BTC per share
- Early-stage nature of the Bitcoin treasury company movement (~300 companies holding BTC, ~20 running the accretive strategy)
- Intangible factors in valuation: leadership quality, marketing velocity, and narrative momentum
- Matt Cole's background at CalPERS and witnessing Federal Reserve debt monetization as a path to Bitcoin conviction
- Risk perception vs. reality in leveraged Bitcoin treasury strategies

**Andrew Webley:**
(0:06:18) So this could be a very long answer, but I'll try and keep it fairly brief. You can get as complicated as you wish, but what I would recommend is that somebody looks at three things. The first one is the mNAV — really easy to work out, you can find it online. Most Bitcoin treasury companies have an analytics dashboard that you can see. What the mNAV means, very simply, is the value of the Bitcoin versus the market cap. Maybe below two is pretty low, below three is relatively low, maybe above five or six is getting expensive. Not financial advice. Then the second thing is the BTC yield. So this is really important. A company like my company will issue equity which increases the shares in issue. In return for doing that, it will generate capital which in our case gets deployed into Bitcoin. So we issue shares and we buy Bitcoin. The growth in that Bitcoin held is the BTC yield — you're calculating the growth in the BTC versus the dilution caused by issuing the equity, which in very simple terms is made possible when the mNAV is greater than one. Then the third thing — something that not a lot of people talk about — is the size of the company. Very simply, the model is ever so slightly easier the smaller the company. So if you look at the market cap of the company — one of the components of your mNAV calculation — you'll be able to see the size, with the theory there being — which isn't 100% true — that the larger you are the harder it is to raise additional capital to make that much difference to your BTC yield and therefore mNAV.
