## Key Topics:
- Introduction of Bitcoin treasury companies: The Smarter Web Company (UK), Strive (US), and Kraken Institutional
- Core KPIs for valuing Bitcoin treasury companies: mNAV, BTC yield, and company size
- Bitcoin as the hurdle rate — if a capital allocation can't beat Bitcoin's expected return, just buy Bitcoin
- Financial engineering tools: convertible notes, ATMs, and the shift toward perpetual preferred equity instruments
- Risk analysis of leverage in Bitcoin treasury companies using fixed income frameworks
- The importance of brand velocity and hitting ATMs early to build the capital flywheel
- The race to $1B scale as a threshold for accessing premium financing and talent
- Shareholder-first approach and transparent risk communication in public Bitcoin companies
- Debate: Ethereum vs Bitcoin as a long-term treasury reserve asset
- Kraken's role as an institutional service provider offering custody, trading, and bespoke lending solutions

**Andrew Webley:**
(0:06:18) So this could be a very long answer, but I'll try and keep it fairly brief. You can get as complicated as you wish, but what I would recommend is that somebody looks at three things. So the first one is the mNAV — really easy to work out, you can find it online. Most Bitcoin treasury companies have an analytics dashboard where you can see it. What the mNAV means, very simply, is the value of the Bitcoin versus the market cap. Maybe below two is pretty low, below three is relatively low, maybe above five or six is getting expensive. Not financial advice. So you've got your mNAV. Then the second thing is the BTC yield. So this is really important. A company like my company will issue equity, which increases the shares in issue. In return for doing that, it will generate capital which in our case gets deployed into Bitcoin. So we issue shares and we buy Bitcoin. So the growth in that Bitcoin held is the BTC yield — you're calculating the growth in the BTC versus the dilution caused by issuing the equity, which in very simple terms is made possible when the mNAV is greater than one. Then the third thing — something that not a lot of people talk about — is the size of the company. Very simply, the model is ever so slightly easier the smaller the company. So if you look at the market cap of the company — one of the components of your mNAV calculation — you'll be able to see the size, with the theory being (which isn't 100% true) that the larger you are, the harder it is to raise additional capital to make that much difference to your BTC yield and therefore mNAV.
