## Key Topics:
- Jesse Myers' journey from altcoiner to Bitcoin maximalist, triggered by the 2018 bear market destroying his altcoin portfolio
- The origin of the X handle "Croesus" — King of Lydia who invented standardized bimetallic coinage 2,600 years ago, as a historical parallel for Bitcoin
- Bitcoin as the digitization of money, analogous to what the internet did for information
- Nick Szabo's "Shelling Out" thesis: shell bead jewelry as prehistoric proof-of-work proto-money, with archaeological evidence now dating back 142,000 years
- Neuroscience discovery: a gene mutation unique to Homo sapiens that supercharged neurogenesis in the frontal lobe (abstract thought region)
- The "Once in a Species" thesis: humanity's competitive advantage over Neanderthals may stem from our brain's capacity for monetary/symbolic behaviour
- Cave paintings and shell jewelry as co-emerging evidence of abstract thought driving early proto-civilisation
- The fiat monetary system as an unexamined default — and Bitcoin as a potential upgrade
- Jesse's definition of crypto: a technological medium enabling value on the internet that can't be stolen or duplicated
- Invitation to a future episode exploring the full history of money

**Jesse Myers:**
(0:05:35) Yes, I would love to. So Croesus was — first of all, he was the King of Lydia 2,600 years ago who came up with the first standardized bimetallic coinage system. So you had your gold coins and your silver coins, and you had the stamp of the king on each coin — and that stamp was attestation that this coin had a certain amount of gold in it. Before that, you didn't have standardized coinage, so if somebody was offering you a bit of metal, you didn't know how much gold you were accepting. There was no way to trust that. But because of this invention — the stamp of the king going on a unit of metal — you could trust that this is a precise amount of gold. And that, if you think about it, is a huge breakthrough in monetary technology, because it reduces the friction involved in trade. You don't have to get out your scales and weigh a bag of gold dust and worry about how much lead is in there. Instead, you can accept the coins of the king with confidence that you're getting a certain amount of gold.
(0:06:48) Saifedean mentions it in "The Bitcoin Standard." I wasn't aware of Croesus until reading that, and it really struck me as the historical analog or parallel for what Bitcoin is doing. Bitcoin is, in my opinion, simply the next big stepping stone in monetary technology evolving — and potentially the final stepping stone — because what Bitcoin represents is going from analog money to digital money. We live in the digital age. We've seen the digitization of information with the internet. And Bitcoin is the digitization of value, the digitization of money. It's analogous to the internet. Bitcoin is doing to the world of value what the internet did to information. 50 years ago you'd go to the library to get all your information or you'd get the newspaper on your doorstep. And now you go to the internet and all information is digitized and accessible anywhere in the world. Bitcoin's doing the same thing to money. You just have to zoom out enough to think about it in historical terms in order for that to be quite clear.
(0:08:05) So I picked that name because it represents to me the right framing for how to think about Bitcoin at a historical level. What I set out to do was build on "Shelling Out" and revisit it. "Shelling Out" was written by Nick Szabo in 2002, so about 25 years ago, and it was all about the anthropological and archaeological evidence of shell bead jewelry. His thesis in that piece was that all of this evidence we see across space and time is not just so that people could look good with fancy jewelry — it was a proof-of-work mechanism that could be used as a sort of proto-money. If you had gone through the effort of collecting shell beads, drilling holes in them, and stringing them up — which would have taken many hours with stone age tools — then you had this valuable good on your person that you could save for a rainy day. When you were in a pinch and needed to exchange some value with another human, your bracelet or necklace would come off, and that was very valuable from an evolutionary perspective. That could save your skin — the difference between surviving and propagating your genes into the future, or not.
