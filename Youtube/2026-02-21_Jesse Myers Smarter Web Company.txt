**Jesse Myers:**
(0:05:35) Yes, I would love to. So Croesus was, well first of all, he was the king of Lydia 2,600 years ago who came up with the first standardized bimetallic coinage system. So you had your gold coins and your silver coins and you had the stamp of the king on each coin and that stamp was attestation that this coin had a certain amount of gold in it. And before that you didn't have standardized coinage. So if somebody was offering you a bit of metal, you didn't know how much gold you were accepting. There was no way to trust that. But because of this invention of the stamp of the king going on this unit of metal, you could trust that this is a precise amount of gold. And that, if you think about it, is a huge breakthrough in monetary technology because it reduces the friction involved in trade. If you want to trade with somebody, you don't have to get out your scales and weigh a bag of gold dust and worry about how much lead is in there. Instead, you can accept the coins of the king with confidence that you're getting a certain amount of gold.

(0:06:46) So, that's a huge milestone. Saifedean mentions it in "The Bitcoin Standard." I wasn't aware of Croesus until reading that and that really struck me as the historical analog or parallel for what Bitcoin is doing. Bitcoin is in my opinion simply the next big stepping stone in monetary technology evolving and potentially the final stepping stone because what Bitcoin represents is going from analog money to digital money. And we live in the digital age. We've seen the digitization of information with the internet. And Bitcoin is the digitization of value, the digitization of money. And it's analogous to the internet. Bitcoin is doing to the world of value what the internet did to information. 50 years ago, you'd go to the library to get all your information or you'd get the newspaper on your doorstep. And now you go to the internet and all information is digitized and accessible anywhere in the world. And we're all quite familiar with how that has revolutionized the world and information access. Bitcoin's doing the same thing to money. You just have to zoom out enough to think about it in historical terms in order for that to be quite clear.

(0:08:02) So, I picked that name because it represents to me the right framing for how to think about Bitcoin at a historical level.
