The main question that people were asking, and where probably the main source of confusion is, is that as we stated there was a large amount of shares that needed to be bought from various index trackers. And the question people have is: where does all that supply actually come from? Prior to the index inclusion, we'd estimated that there were probably around 12 to 13 million shares that needed to be bought. And so people were naturally asking, right, so who's actually going to be selling those shares? The selling of the shares comes from the market. And what that means is there are really four different buckets of investors that effectively look to participate in these types of auctions: active managers, the index funds that Jesse mentioned, retail holders, and some of these what we'd call special situation type funds as well. They all amalgamate together to make the majority of the supply. But really where it comes from — like you said — is that there are a variety of different hedge funds whose job it is to effectively trade around these strategies. They will start to accumulate positions in stocks that need to be added prior to any index inclusion, and they'll look to sell shares in stocks that will be deleted from various indexes as well. When they start those positions is really TBD — it depends on what the market is doing, what the individual stock is doing, how big the index inclusion or deletion is, etc. A good example: let's say hypothetically there was an oil company that was going to be excluded and there was a lot of news going around on the oil price and geopolitics — somebody may take a specific view on that stock based on that news depending on when they would like to start that order. That's where the majority of the flow comes from, and there are a lot of desks across the hedge fund industry that do this globally — it's a really big business.
