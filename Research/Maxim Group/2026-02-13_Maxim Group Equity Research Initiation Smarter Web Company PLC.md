## Bitcoin Background and Businesses

### Bitcoin

Bitcoin, launched in 2009, is unique among digital assets: it is the largest by market value, has substantial transaction volume, and is arguably the most widespread mindshare in the market, in our opinion. We believe BTC has developed a global appeal.

Bitcoin is a decentralized ledger used to create a cryptocurrency built on open-source software. There is no central authority, instead relying on software and peer-to-peer consensus derived from a proof-of-work process to establish and authenticate transactions. A white paper titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System" was published by Satoshi Nakamoto on a technical email list in 2008 and described how bitcoin would function. Satoshi subsequently released the bitcoin software in January 2009 and disappeared in 2010. Satoshi's true identity and whereabouts remain a subject of speculation and rumor. He is thought to have mined roughly 1M Bitcoin in the early days of the network, representing about 5% of the total supply.

The network operates through a few (simplified) functions. New BTC is mined approximately every 10 minutes and issued as a reward to miners that "solve" a computationally intensive task. There is a finite number of BTC that will ever be issued (~21M). Bitcoin miners also process transactions by solving computationally intensive tasks and may receive transaction fees for doing so. BTC wallets have private and public keys to address, sign, and validate the transfer of bitcoin between owners. All transfers of BTC are recorded on the blockchain, which maintains the history of all prior ownership. In other words, each transaction or transfer of ownership is encoded into the blockchain and broadcast to the network. Bitcoin miners' use of computational power to validate transactions is called proof-of-work.

Approximately 19.9M BTC have been issued to date, out of a total of 21M that will be issued by the year 2140. The number of new BTC issued as a reward to miners is cut in half every four years. The reward was 50 at the inception of the network and dropped to 3.125 in April 2024.

By virtue of there being a limited number of BTC that will ever be issued, the cryptocurrency is structurally different from traditional fiat currencies issued by governments. Whereas the U.S., for example, creates new currency through the fractional reserve banking system — which leads to a steady expansion of the money supply — BTC cannot significantly dilute itself due to its limited supply.

Early use of BTC as a currency or transaction medium gained notoriety through its association with illicit activities, particularly following the emergence of an online marketplace known as Silk Road in 2011. Silk Road was built on Tor, a software-driven network that encrypts internet traffic. Transactions on Silk Road were conducted using bitcoin to maintain anonymity, and the platform was accessible only through the Tor network, obscuring it from conventional internet browsing. Research into the Silk Road marketplace found that approximately 70% of products listed for sale were illicit drugs. The platform had roughly 100K buyers and generated nearly $200M in sales during its almost three years of operation. The founder was arrested, tried, and convicted, and was serving two life sentences prior to receiving a pardon from President Trump in January 2025. Silk Road was one of several instances in which bitcoin became entangled in illicit activity.

Despite these challenges, the SEC approved U.S.-listed exchange traded funds (ETFs) to track BTC beginning in January 2024, following a U.S. Court of Appeals decision that found the SEC had failed to adequately explain its reasoning in rejecting the listing and trading of Grayscale's proposed ETF. After rejecting approximately 20 filings since 2018, the SEC adjusted its position in 2024, and as of January 2025, approximately 25 ETFs are available in the U.S. with direct BTC exposure and ~125 globally. These products provide exposure to BTC from sponsors such as Fidelity, Invesco (IVZ - NR), ProShares, and BlackRock (BLK - NR).

Once a niche asset with significant barriers to entry for the average investor, spot bitcoin ETFs were approved in early 2024 and now total ~$84B in assets. We believe that increasing awareness, acceptance, and access — combined with bitcoin's finite issuance — support the long-term outperformance of bitcoin as an asset.

Then-President-elect Donald Trump met with bitcoin mining executives in June 2024 and communicated support for digital assets throughout his campaign. Appointments to regulatory bodies have been viewed as crypto-friendly, and President Trump has advocated for the establishment of a strategic BTC reserve.

The culmination of BTC's growing prominence — including a sovereign nation adopting bitcoin as legal tender, substantial asset flow into bitcoin ETFs since their approval, and numerous public companies announcing crypto assets as a treasury strategy — suggests substantial global momentum.

## Treasury Rationale

Bitcoin's short history and volatility have led some to question its reliability as a long-term inflation hedge. However, we note that >95% of all BTC that will ever be issued is already in circulation. The total supply of bitcoin in circulation will grow by about 0.9% per year (~164,250 BTC added to the supply annually), until the next halving event in 2028, when annual issuance will decline to around 82,000, or around 0.4% per year. By comparison, the long-term growth rate of the U.S. money supply has been around 7%.

We believe BTC is attractive and resilient for several core reasons:

1. Proof-of-work is a transparent, effective method for validating transactions and preventing centralized control of the network.
2. BTC benefits from a first-mover advantage and has a market cap of $1.3T as of 2/4/26, ~5x greater than that of the next-largest cryptocurrency, Ethereum. While there appears to be meaningful appetite for cryptocurrencies in general, BTC continues to command the majority of market momentum and adoption.
3. The diminishing issuance of new bitcoin and its finite total supply suggest a potentially deflationary monetary asset.
4. The global distribution of bitcoin miners, increasing adoption, and inherently decentralized architecture make bitcoin difficult — and potentially unlikely — to be broken by government regulation.

Bitcoin is often seen as a hedge against inflation because of its limited supply and controlled monetary policy. With a cap of 21M coins versus ~19.9M currently in circulation, BTC ensures long-term scarcity, protecting it from the kind of continuous inflation that can erode the value of traditional currencies. Unlike the U.S. dollar, which can be weakened through excessive issuance, bitcoin's supply is regulated by a decentralized system with a predictable halving schedule that slows the release of new coins over time. This mechanism mirrors the characteristics of gold, making BTC a potential store of value during periods of inflation.

Much like counter-inflationary assets such as gold and real estate, BTC's limited supply and implied scarcity should result in increased purchasing power relative to the U.S. dollar and other traditional currencies over time. We estimate BTC's $1.3T valuation is about 4% of gold's. The combination of BTC's increasing relative value versus the U.S. dollar due to money supply growth, and the potential for more than a 5x inflow of assets into bitcoin over time as a safe haven and / or inflation hedge, should support long-term performance.

Bitcoin's scarcity may also be magnified by the propensity of holders to treat the asset like digital gold. In other words, BTC treasury companies generally have no intention of selling the BTC they accumulate and expect the asset to outperform alternatives over time. Some coins are forever lost to wallets residing on computers that were scrapped or destroyed, with no mechanism for the network to return the virtually owned coins to circulation. In addition, some investment platforms, such as Fidelity, recommend including crypto assets as a component of retirement portfolios, which we believe may lock up additional bitcoin for decades.

BTC treasury companies measure their cost of capital against the expected future return of holding BTC. MCap/NAV, defined as market cap divided by the value of BTC holdings, is used to determine whether issuing common stock to acquire bitcoin will be accretive to BTC per share. SWC currently trades at ~0.5x mNAV, meaning shares trade at a discount to BTC holdings, and issuing stock to acquire BTC decreases the ratio of BTC per share. However, even at this multiple to NAV, we believe the company can respond to intraday volatility of the stock and BTC, when conditions become favorable.

The company's Bitcoin treasury strategy largely hinges on the view that conservative cash or debt treasury holdings will fail to keep pace with inflation, providing structural support for BTC to outperform traditionally conservative assets. Meanwhile, as digital assets — particularly bitcoin — become more broadly and globally accessible and accepted by regulators and investors, capital could accumulate to levels comparable with other safe-haven assets such as gold.

## P/BYD Ratio

In support of developing valuation metrics for bitcoin treasury companies, SWC's Head of Bitcoin Strategy, Jesse Myers, created the P/BYD (price / bitcoin yield delivered) ratio, which is intended to convey information similar to a P/E ratio for traditional equities.

Bitcoin yield delivered, in the context of BTC treasury companies, is generated by increasing BTC holdings per share. This can be achieved through "accretive dilution," where a company issues new equity at a premium to its net asset value (NAV) for the purpose of purchasing bitcoin. At a premium mNAV, the increase in bitcoin per share from an equity issuance is larger than the percentage of dilution in the stock. Other tools include raising capital via convertible debt, ordinary debt, or preferred equity.

Current valuation methods include mNAV, defined as market cap divided by the value of treasury assets (cash + BTC), and days to cover, which represents the number of days since the company's BTC per share was equal to the current BTC per share, divided by the current mNAV.

The goal of P/BYD is to combine both elements (current market premium and recent rate of performance) to estimate how long it may take for investors to earn back the value of their investment. The ratio evaluates how long it will take to deliver sufficient BTC yield to offset the mNAV premium paid upfront.

Pictured below is the formula used to calculate P/BYD. The variables are defined as follows:

- y = years to shareholder return on bitcoin held
- mNAV = multiple of net asset value (market cap/treasury assets)
- BYD = bitcoin yield delivered over the most recent period
- n = number of periods per year

### P/BYD Ratio Formula

```
y = log(mNAV) / (n * log(1 + BYD))
```

Because of the exponential nature of compounding BTC yield over successive periods, isolating the years to shareholder return on bitcoin held requires logarithmic terms. BYD (bitcoin yield delivered) should be from the most recent period, which may be one year, one quarter, or even one month. Adjusting *n* to account for the number of periods in a year normalizes the length of period used. All else equal, longer periods are likely to be more representative. However, for less mature BTC treasury companies, a shorter period may better reflect the current ability of a BTC treasury company to generate additional BTC yield.

P/BYD is similar to days to cover, but differs in that it: 1) returns a result expressed in years, and 2) allows the user to define a recent performance period to project forward, rather than simply looking back in time as far as necessary.

The ratio assumes a continued rate of return consistent with the most recent period used. The result is an estimate, expressed in years, of how long it would take investors to earn cumulative BTC equal to their investment today.

## Risks

**Digital assets are volatile instruments.** Bitcoin prices are highly volatile and can experience substantial fluctuations over short periods. Factors such as market sentiment, regulatory developments, macroeconomic conditions, and technological changes in the cryptocurrency ecosystem can contribute to rapid price changes. A significant decline in BTC prices can result in material losses for holders, reduce the value of investments linked to BTC, and create uncertainty for participants in the market. This volatility makes bitcoin a high-risk asset, and price declines can occur suddenly and unpredictably.

**Regulation is an evolving landscape.** Regulatory oversight of digital asset businesses varies by state and country. In the United States, digital assets may fall under the oversight of multiple federal and state agencies, depending on their characterization. For example, the SEC continues to pursue enforcement actions based on the view that many digital assets qualify as investment contracts, while the CFTC has asserted that bitcoin and ether are commodities. The European Union recently adopted the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) Regulation to establish a harmonized regulatory regime across member states.

**The company's assets will be concentrated in BTC.** This concentration exposes the firm to the volatility of the BTC market, as fluctuations in price can have a significant impact on the value of its holdings. The firm's strategy, including its bitcoin treasury approach, reflects a deliberate focus on acquiring and holding BTC as a core asset, which increases potential gains in rising markets but also amplifies risks if prices decline.

**The Smarter Web Company's BTC strategy has not been tested.** The company's model centers on using capital raised through private investments, public offerings, and strategic partnerships to acquire BTC directly. While this approach is designed to grow the company's bitcoin holdings and potentially generate returns, it has not been implemented at scale, and its success remains unproven, particularly in the face of bitcoin's price volatility, regulatory developments, or operational challenges.

**The Smarter Web Company's proposed BTC strategy may face heightened regulatory scrutiny.** We believe the company is the only LSE-listed BTC digital asset treasury (DAT). As regulation of digital assets evolves, The Smarter Web Company could encounter additional oversight or restrictions that affect its ability to implement and expand its bitcoin-focused strategy, potentially creating operational, legal, and financial risks.

**Growth of other digital assets.** There are numerous alternative digital assets, and many entities, including consortiums and financial institutions, are researching and investing resources into private or permissioned blockchain platforms or digital assets that do not use proof-of-work mining like the BTC network. Stablecoins are another category of digital assets that compete with BTC and are designed to maintain a consistent price issuers' commitments to hold high-quality liquid assets equal to the total value of stablecoins in circulation. Stablecoins have grown rapidly as an alternative to BTC and other digital assets as a medium of exchange and store of value. In addition, central banks in some countries have started to introduce digital forms of legal tender, while others are exploring the potential creation of central bank digital currencies (CBDCs). As a result, the emergence or growth of these or other digital assets could cause the market price of BTC to decline, which could have a material adverse effect on The Smarter Web Company's financial condition and operating results.

**Emerging quantum computing technology poses a theoretical threat to bitcoin.** Emerging quantum computing technology poses a theoretical risk to BTC because advances in quantum processing could eventually undermine the cryptographic algorithms that secure the network. Bitcoin relies on elliptic curve cryptography (ECC) to protect private keys and validate transactions, and experts note that sufficiently powerful quantum computers could, in theory, break ECC by rapidly solving the underlying mathematical problems that make it secure. While current quantum capabilities are far from being able to compromise BTC's network at scale, the possibility of future breakthroughs creates a long-term risk. BTC's decentralized governance model makes significant protocol changes slow, contentious, and potentially impossible without significant fragmentation of the community. For The Smarter Web Company, whose strategy involves concentrating assets in BTC, any significant progress in quantum computing could raise concerns about the durability of BTC's security model and potentially impact investor confidence, even before such threats are technically viable.
